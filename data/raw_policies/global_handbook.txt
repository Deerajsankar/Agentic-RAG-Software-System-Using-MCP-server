# ExampleCorp Global Employee Handbook (Enterprise Edition)

**Effective date:** 2026-03-17  
**Document owner:** People Ops (Global)  
**Applies to:** All employees, contractors (where referenced), interns, and contingent workers with access to ExampleCorp systems  

> This handbook is a consolidated enterprise policy baseline. **Local addenda and country-specific rules prevail** where required by law or collective agreements. Where this handbook is stricter than local law, the stricter standard applies **unless prohibited** by law. Where local law is stricter, local law applies.

---

## 1. Guiding principles and “how we operate”

### 1.1 Our operating posture (corporate reality)
- **Compliance-first:** We optimize for auditability, regulatory defensibility, and consistent outcomes across jurisdictions.
- **Least privilege:** Access to data and systems is provided strictly on a need-to-know basis and may be revoked without notice.
- **Documented decisions:** If it’s not recorded in the HRIS/ticketing system, it did not happen (for compliance purposes).
- **One company, many jurisdictions:** Regional deviations exist and must be applied correctly; **regional filtering is mandatory**.

### 1.2 Definitions (used across policies)
| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Employee** | An individual hired by an ExampleCorp legal entity and on payroll. |
| **Contingent Worker** | Contractor, vendor staff, agency worker, or consultant. |
| **People Ops** | HR function (including HRBP, Benefits, Payroll, Mobility, ER). |
| **Manager** | Any employee with people-management responsibilities (direct or dotted line). |
| **Confidential Data** | Any non-public data including HR data, compensation, customer info, source code, security artifacts. |

## 2. Code of conduct and workplace standards

### 2.1 Professional conduct
We expect behavior that is lawful, respectful, and aligned with company values. The following are **non-exhaustive** examples of prohibited conduct:
1. Harassment, discrimination, retaliation, or bullying (including “subtle” harassment and microaggressions).
2. Misuse of company funds, falsification of records, or “creative accounting.”
3. Unauthorized disclosure of Confidential Data (including compensation, performance notes, immigration status).
4. Circumventing security controls “for convenience.”

### 2.2 Reporting channels and non-retaliation
- **Primary route:** Manager → People Ops → Legal/Compliance (as applicable)
- **Alternative routes:** Ethics hotline, anonymous reporting portal, Security incident channel for data/system concerns
- **Non-retaliation:** Any perceived retaliation will be investigated as a standalone policy violation.

### 2.3 Conflicts of interest (COI)
- Disclose outside employment, board roles, family reporting lines, and vendor relationships.
- If you influence purchasing decisions, you must disclose **any** personal interest.
- **Edge case:** If a spouse/partner joins as a vendor employee, you must not be involved in vendor selection, contract negotiation, or invoice approvals.

## 3. Employment lifecycle (global baseline)

### 3.1 Hiring, background checks, and eligibility
- Offers are contingent on verification (identity, education/employment where legally permitted, references, sanctions screening).
- Employment eligibility documentation must be provided within local statutory timelines.
- **Edge case:** If verification results conflict with self-reported information, People Ops may pause onboarding, restrict system access, or rescind the offer consistent with law.

### 3.2 Working hours, attendance, and “time integrity”
- Employees must comply with local working time rules and accurately report attendance where required.
- **No off-the-clock work** where prohibited by law (especially relevant for hourly/non-exempt populations).
- **Edge case:** Cross-time-zone teams must not create an expectation of constant availability; managers must manage coverage explicitly.

### 3.3 Remote work and location integrity
- Your work location must match the approved location in HRIS.
- Working from a different country/state without authorization can create tax, payroll, immigration, and export-control risk.
- **Strict rule:** Any cross-border work beyond **10 business days** in a rolling 90-day period requires Mobility approval.

## 4. Time off, leaves, and holidays (global baseline)

### 4.1 PTO and leave hierarchy
Time off requests follow this hierarchy:
1. **Statutory leaves** (parental, sick, bereavement, civic duty) as required by law
2. **Company PTO policies** (vacation, personal days)
3. **Manager discretion** (team-specific downtime scheduling)

### 4.2 Requesting time off
- All time off must be recorded in the HRIS **before the leave begins** (or as soon as practicable for emergencies).
- Managers must approve/deny within **5 business days**. Non-response is not approval.
- **Blackout periods:** Certain roles (Payroll, IT Security, Incident Response) may have blackout periods. Managers must publish them quarterly.

### 4.3 Sick leave integrity and privacy
- Do not disclose medical details to managers; provide only what is necessary (dates, ability to work, restrictions).
- People Ops may request certification where permitted by law, with a privacy-minimizing approach.

## 5. Data privacy and employee records

### 5.1 Employee data handling
- HR data is **Highly Confidential** and may only be accessed by authorized roles.
- Access is monitored, logged, and periodically reviewed.
- **Edge case:** Managers should not store performance notes in personal documents; use approved systems only.

### 5.2 Records retention (high level)
Retention varies by jurisdiction. If unsure, treat as:
- **Recruiting records:** 2–7 years depending on region
- **Payroll/tax:** 7–10 years
- **Performance/ER:** 3–7 years

## 6. Regional addenda (apply the correct region)

> **Important:** The same topic may have different requirements. When applying this handbook, always start with the employee’s **legal entity + work location** in HRIS.

### 6.1 United States (USA)
#### 6.1.1 Classification and overtime
- Employees may be classified as **Exempt** or **Non-Exempt** based on role duties and salary thresholds.
- **Non-Exempt:** Overtime must be pre-approved; all hours worked must be recorded; managers may discipline for unauthorized overtime but must still pay for hours worked.

#### 6.1.2 Leave highlights
- **FMLA (where applicable):** Eligible employees may take job-protected leave; People Ops administers eligibility and documentation.
- **State-specific rules:** Certain states mandate paid sick leave accrual, pay transparency, and final pay timing.

#### 6.1.3 At-will employment (where applicable)
Employment may be at-will except where prohibited by contract or law. This does not override anti-discrimination obligations.

### 6.2 India
#### 6.2.1 Working time and holidays
- Working hours and holiday calendars vary by state and establishment registration.
- Attendance and leave must be recorded accurately; payroll compliance relies on HRIS records.

#### 6.2.2 Statutory components and separation
- Statutory benefits may include PF/ESI (where applicable), professional tax, and gratuity eligibility (subject to tenure and law).
- **Notice period:** Notice period obligations are governed by employment terms and local law; **buyout** may be permitted per contract and policy.

#### 6.2.3 Data handling
- Sensitive personal data must be handled per applicable Indian data protection requirements and company security standards.

### 6.3 United Kingdom (UK)
#### 6.3.1 Working time regulations
- Working time limits apply unless an employee has signed a valid opt-out (where permitted).
- Rest breaks and holiday entitlements must meet statutory minimums.

#### 6.3.2 Sick pay and absence
- Statutory Sick Pay (SSP) rules apply where eligible; company sick pay (if offered) is administered per plan design.
- Fit notes may be required depending on absence duration.

#### 6.3.3 Right to work
Right to work checks must be completed in the prescribed manner and timeframe.

### 6.4 Germany
#### 6.4.1 Works council / co-determination (where applicable)
- Certain changes (working time, monitoring tools, policy rollouts) may require works council consultation.
- Managers must engage People Ops/Legal before implementing changes affecting German employees.

#### 6.4.2 Vacation and sickness
- Statutory vacation minimums apply; vacation is generally intended to be taken within the applicable period.
- Medical certificates may be required according to German practice and company process.

#### 6.4.3 Data protection (GDPR)
- Employee data processing must follow GDPR principles: lawfulness, purpose limitation, data minimization, retention controls, and rights handling.

## 7. Disciplinary process (enterprise baseline)

### 7.1 Progressive discipline (where lawful)
Depending on severity and jurisdiction, steps may include:
1. Verbal warning (documented)
2. Written warning
3. Performance Improvement Plan (PIP) or final warning
4. Termination

**Immediate action** may occur for severe misconduct (violence, serious data breach, fraud).

### 7.2 Investigations
- Investigations are confidential, time-bound, and documented.
- Employees must cooperate; obstruction may be a policy violation.
- **Edge case:** If an employee is a key witness but requests anonymity, People Ops will assess feasibility; anonymity cannot be guaranteed.
